Let's start with the most important aspect of wireless networks: Their standard.

Standards are absolutely important to make sure networks can communicate with each other. The IEEE 802 family deals with local and metropolian area networks, with Ethernet, WLAN, Bluetooth and Zigbee as well-known examples.

IEEE 802.11 is a set of medium access control (MAC) and physical layer (PHY) specifications for implementing wireless local area network (WLAN) computer communication in the 2.4, 3.6, 5 and 60 GHz frequency bands.

Source: Wikipedia

This is how Wikipedia defines IEEE 802.11.

Most common devices you can get today are 2.4 and 5 GHz respectively. 3.6 GHz devices are rare and may only be operated in the USA with a license. The 802.11ad standard, which is under development, will allow 60 GHz operation with up to 7 Gbit/s, but only in line-of-sight.

runs any ISO/OSI Layer3 protocol
IPv4 IPv6
ICMP IPX IGMP EGP IPSec

A very important fact is that WLAN operates only at the first two ISO/OSI layers, which means that you can bridge it with your regular ethernet without the need for a router between your network segments. So even your 1999 vintage 802.11b wireless access point can pass through IPv6 traffic if you bridge your WLAN to your ethernet.

Receiver Sensitivity

How good your router can "hear".

Depends on modulation and bit rate and your router hardware.

Receiver Sensitivity


Example: Ubiquiti Bullet M RX Sensitivity

Signal to Noise Ratio


  • EM noise is usually at around -90dB to -110dB
    (but only displayed by some drivers)
  • Signal Strength - Noise Level = Signal Margin
  • > 40dB: excellent signal
  • < 10dB: weak link

Example:
(-40dBm) - (-100dBm) = 60dB Signal Margin

Why does my device get disconnected with 20dB signal margin?

  • Good signal margins required on both devices
  • Signal level shows only what's being received
